# The Authentic Source Checklist

> *A QC checklist for verifying primary data sources before they enter a briefing.*

## The principle

Market intelligence is only as authentic as its weakest source. A single fabricated statistic, a single secondary source masquerading as primary, a single AI-summarized data point with no underlying citation — and the entire brief becomes suspect. This checklist exists to keep that from happening.

## The eight checks

Run every data point in your brief through the following eight questions. If a data point fails any of them, it does not enter the brief.

### 1. Is the source the original publisher?

A statistic about supplier market share should come from the supplier's annual report, the regulator, or a recognized index — not from a blog summarizing a report summarizing the original. Trace every number to its origin.

### 2. Is the source dated?

If the data point has no publication date, it does not belong in a briefing. Procurement decisions are time-sensitive. A 2019 market share figure presented as current is not authentic.

### 3. Is the source authoritative for this specific claim?

A consulting firm publishing a thought-leadership piece on chip supply is not authoritative for chip supply data. The semiconductor industry association is. Match the source to the claim.

### 4. Is there a second independent source that confirms it?

For any quantitative claim that materially affects the recommendation, a second independent source must agree within a reasonable margin. If you cannot find a second source, mark the data point as "single-source" in the brief.

### 5. Has the source been quoted accurately?

Read the original quote in full. Make sure the data point in your brief is not stripped of qualifying context, time horizon, or methodology footnotes.

### 6. Is the methodology disclosed?

Authentic sources disclose how the number was produced — sample size, geography, time window, definitions. Sources that publish numbers without methodology should be cited with a caveat or excluded.

### 7. Is the source free from obvious commercial bias?

A vendor publishing a study about its own product category is not a primary source. Use it only when triangulated with a non-commercial source.

### 8. Would I defend this source in front of the CFO?

The final test. If the answer is no, the data point does not enter the brief.

## Workflow

Before writing the brief, list every claim you intend to make in a single column. In a second column, paste the URL or full citation of the source. In a third column, mark the result of each check with ✓ or ✗. Any row with even one ✗ is either rewritten or removed.

This checklist is slow. It is also the difference between market intelligence and market opinion.
