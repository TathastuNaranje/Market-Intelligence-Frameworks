# The Negotiation Brief Outline

> *A pre-meeting structure for sourcing managers preparing for high-stakes negotiations.*

## When to use

Use this outline before any negotiation that involves multi-year commitments, multi-million dollar spend, or single-source supplier relationships. For tactical negotiations, this is overkill — for strategic ones, this is the minimum.

## The seven sections

### 1. The objective (one sentence)

Write the single sentence that defines what success looks like at the end of the meeting. Not a range of acceptable outcomes — one sentence. Example: *"Secure a 12% rate reduction with a 24-month commitment, no auto-renewal, and a quarterly volume review clause."*

### 2. The walk-away (one sentence)

Write the single sentence that defines the point at which you walk away from the table. This is your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) translated into a tripwire. Example: *"Anything less than an 8% reduction with the volume review clause means we move 30% of spend to Supplier B by Q4."*

### 3. The opening offer (one sentence)

Write the exact opening you will state. Practice saying it aloud. The opening should be aggressive enough to give you room to concede, but defensible enough that you can back it up with data if challenged.

### 4. The supplier's likely position (one paragraph)

Predict the supplier's opening, their concerns, their constraints, and their internal pressures. The better you predict their position, the less you will be surprised in the room. If you cannot predict it, you are not ready to negotiate.

### 5. The concession ladder (a table)

Plan exactly which concessions you are willing to make, in what order, and what you expect in return for each. A concession given without a corresponding ask is a gift, not a negotiation.

| Concession Level | What You Give | What You Get |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Extend term to 36 months | 14% rate reduction |
| 2 | Add a volume guarantee | 16% rate reduction |
| 3 | Single-source 60% of category | 18% rate reduction + dedicated CSM |

### 6. The questions you will ask (a list)

List the five to seven questions you will ask in the meeting. Good questions surface information that strengthens your position. Great questions reframe the supplier's perception of the deal.

### 7. The post-meeting actions (a list)

Before you walk in, write the actions that must follow the meeting regardless of outcome — internal stakeholder updates, legal review of any verbal commitments, follow-up email to the supplier with the agreed terms. The negotiation does not end when the meeting ends.

## The rehearsal

Read the outline aloud the night before the meeting. Walk through your opening, your concession ladder, and your walk-away. If any sentence feels uncertain when spoken, rewrite it. You are not preparing slides — you are rehearsing a performance.
