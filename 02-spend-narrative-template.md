# The Spend Narrative Template

> *How to structure a category brief so that it reads like a story — and drives action.*

## Why narrative matters

A category brief that reads like a spreadsheet gets filed. A category brief that reads like a story gets acted on. Sourcing managers do not lack data; they lack narrative. This template provides a repeatable structure for turning numbers into a decision-grade story.

## The five-part structure

### 1. The protagonist (one paragraph)

Who is the buyer in this category, and what do they actually want? Not what the budget says they want — what the spend pattern reveals they want. Write this as a character study.

### 2. The setting (one paragraph)

What is the market context? Macro pressure, supplier consolidation, raw material trends, regulatory shifts. Establish the world before introducing conflict.

### 3. The conflict (two paragraphs)

What is the tension? This is almost always a gap — between budget and actual spend, between intent and execution, between current panel and capability needed, between price paid and market benchmark. Quantify the gap, but frame it as a problem the protagonist is facing, not a number on a slide.

### 4. The resolution (two paragraphs)

What does the next chapter look like? The proposed sourcing action. Be specific about what changes, who acts, what the expected outcome is, and on what timeline. The resolution is not a recommendation — it is a script for the protagonist's next move.

### 5. The stakes (one paragraph)

What happens if no action is taken? What is the cost of staying in the current chapter? This is the line that converts a brief from informational to actionable.

## Length and form

A spend narrative brief is exactly **one page** when printed. If it requires more, the story is not yet sharp enough. Use the appendix for tables, charts, and supporting data — but the narrative itself stays on one page.

## Tone

Write it in clear, declarative English. Avoid procurement jargon when narrative language will do. The brief will be read by stakeholders who do not speak procurement — write for them.

## Test

Before sending the brief, read it aloud. If you can read it as a coherent story without losing the audience, it works. If it sounds like a list of bullet points strung together, rewrite.
